# Personal Purchase Decision Matrix — Requirements & Reasoning

## Purpose

This isn't a budget calculator, and it isn't a set of hard rules. It's a tool meant to externalize a set of checks that are easy to skip in the actual moment of wanting something — because the goal is to interrupt the in-the-moment, "decide now, think later" pattern, not to enforce restraint for its own sake. Every check below traces back to a real, named difference between purchases that turned out right and ones that didn't.

## Design principles

- No hard numeric gates (e.g. "if debt > $X, block purchase"). Hard stops don't work for this user — they get bypassed or resented rather than followed.
- The trigger — excitement, longing, imagining a future with the item — is constant across both good and bad purchases. It is not itself diagnostic and should never be treated as a red flag on its own.
- The matrix exists specifically because, in the moment, the checks below get skipped. Its only job is to be the pause that the moment otherwise removes.
- Every check is built around a *felt* signal, not an abstract one. Numbers alone ("save $X") don't reliably motivate; a feeling tied to a real consequence does.
- Self-reported answers (track record, feel check, etc.) are never gatekept or verified live — the tool should never delay or withhold guidance to force honesty in the moment, since that just leads to the tool being abandoned. Instead, whatever is self-reported gets taken at face value and logged; calibration happens separately, later, with distance — which is where this particular user's self-awareness is actually reliable.

## Stage 1 — Feel the pull (entry point)

**What it captures:** the moment something lights up — excitement, longing, or picturing a future with it.

**Reasoning:** this stage is intentionally not a decision point. It marks where the flow begins, nothing more. No branching logic here — it flows straight into the pace check.

## Stage 2 — Pace check

This stage exists because the felt urgency to decide immediately is almost always false, and what's underneath it is a discomfort with waiting rather than real scarcity.

**2a. Feels urgent?** — Does it feel like a decision has to be made right now, before leaving or before some window closes?
- Yes → go to 2b.
- No → skip ahead directly to Stage 3 (Realness check).

**2b. Scarcity check** — Is the scarcity actually real (limited stock, final day, genuinely won't be available again), or is this just impatience?
- Real → skip ahead to Stage 3.
- Just impatience → go to 2c.

**2c. Create space (action)** — Deliberately delay: leave, sleep on it, revisit later. Naming the impatience explicitly — rather than letting it pass for urgency about the item — is often enough on its own to loosen its grip. This is a pause, not a refusal; the flow continues once revisited.
- → continues to Stage 3.

## Stage 3 — Realness check

Liking a brand or category (Fuji lenses, Lululemon) doesn't reliably predict liking a specific item — taste turns out to be specific, not general. This stage checks whether the want is grounded in real evidence about *this* thing, not assumed from category affinity.

**3a. Track record check** — Is there real history with this *exact* thing — not the brand, not the category, but this specific item or experience?
- Strong history → skip ahead to Stage 4 (Fit check); no further testing needed, since deep history already answers the question accurately.
- No history → go to 3b.

**3b. Tested check** — Has this specific item actually been tried — rented, borrowed, or learned about from someone who's used it — rather than only imagined? This question has three possible answers, not two:
- Already tested → go to 3c.
- Not yet tested, but testing is genuinely possible (could rent, try in-store, ask someone, wait it out) → outcome: **Not yet**. This is a pause, not a no — find a way to test before deciding (try it, borrow it, ask someone, or let time pass and see if the want survives without the heat of the moment).
- Genuinely cannot be tested at all — no brick-and-mortar option, no rental, no way to trial it before buying → go to 3b-i.

**3b-i. Gladness check (for genuinely untestable items only)** — *If this doesn't turn out the way I'm hoping — am I still glad I have it?* This isn't asking whether the item will perform well or get used; it's asking whether the relationship with having it can survive disappointment. It exists specifically for the case where testing is structurally impossible, not for cases where testing was simply skipped.
- Yes, still glad either way → continue to 3c, treated the same as a tested item that produced joy.
- No → does not fail to **Don't buy**. Instead, this is a hard requirement: a way to test this must be found before deciding, even if it takes more effort than usual (sourcing a secondhand unit to try, finding someone who owns one, traveling to see it in person, etc.). The lack of testing options doesn't override the need for real evidence — it just means more creativity is needed to get it.

**3c. Feel check** — Did testing actually produce joy, or was the result just neutral ("don't particularly like or dislike it")?
- Neutral or worse → outcome: **Don't buy**. Neutral is not a mild positive — it's the same signal that predicted real regret before.
- Joy → continue to Stage 4.

## Stage 4 — Fit check

Even once a want is confirmed real, several things can still make it wrong — and each needs a felt or behavioral translation, since abstract numbers ("don't overspend," "save for later") don't carry enough weight to be followed through on.

**4a. Habit check** — Would this require a new habit to actually use, or does it fit a frequency already lived at?
- Reasoning: loving something doesn't guarantee it gets used. The clear failure case is something that needs a usage pattern that doesn't already exist — a shampoo for once-a-month use when hair is washed daily, for instance. This doesn't penalize categories where feeling alone is the point (display items) or where a new item slots into an already-existing daily habit (everyday clothing) — in both cases the honest answer naturally comes out as "fits."
- Needs a new habit → outcome: **Don't buy**.
- Fits an existing frequency → go to 4b.

**4b. Replacement check** — Is this meant to replace something already owned?
- Reasoning: a purchase justified as "this will replace X" often gets the space question answered as if the replacement has already happened, when it's still just a plan. The space math only holds if the old thing actually leaves.
- No → skip ahead to 4d (Space check).
- Yes → go to 4c.

**4c. Already-gone check** — Has that replacement actually happened (sold, given away, genuinely stopped using), or is it still just planned?
- Both answers continue to the space check, but the assumption carried forward differs: already gone means the freed-up space can be counted; still planned means the space check must be answered as if nothing has left yet.

**4d. Space check** — Would this overflow the space made for it — would something have to be compromised to fit it in? (Answered using whichever assumption was set in 4b/4c — never crediting space that hasn't actually been freed.)
- Overflows → outcome: **Don't buy**.
- Fits → go to 4e.

**4e. Cycle check** — Would this deepen the stuckness — extend the feeling of being unable to get out of the current cycle?
- Deepens it → outcome: **Don't buy**.
- Stays clear → outcome: **Buy it**.

## Final outcomes

- **Buy it** — every check passed: a real, grounded want, fitting an existing habit, with no felt cost in space or in the cycle.
- **Don't buy** — failed at the realness check (no joy), or at the fit check (a new habit would be needed, the space math doesn't hold once the replacement assumption is corrected for, or the cycle deepens).
- **Not yet** — not quite a single outcome anymore. For items that *could* be tested but haven't been, this interrupts the old "buy now, think later" default and redirects toward finding a way to gain real information first — and this case is allowed to sit unresolved indefinitely. For items that genuinely *can't* be tested and also fail the gladness check, "not yet" becomes a firmer requirement to find some way to test, however unconventional, before deciding.

## Notes for implementation

- The track record question must be scoped to the exact item or experience, not answerable from brand or category alone — this distinction is the most common point of failure in past decisions.
- No dollar amount or budget threshold should be hardcoded anywhere as a blocking condition. The fit check substitutes felt and behavioral proxies (habit fit, space overflow, cycle stuckness) for numeric limits by design.
- Log every self-reported answer (track record, feel check, habit fit, etc.) alongside the eventual outcome. This is not meant to verify honesty in the moment — that approach was considered and rejected, since gatekeeping live answers would just lead to the tool being abandoned. The log exists so calibration can happen later, with distance: reviewing whether things tagged "strong track record" or "joy" actually held up is where this user's self-awareness is reliable, and the tool should support that review rather than trying to enforce honesty live.
